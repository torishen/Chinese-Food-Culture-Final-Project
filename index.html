Eating Alone Together
For Chinese international students in the U.S., how does food function as a way of maintaining cultural identity and navigating belonging?
Scroll to begin
The Project
When you cross a border,
your body doesn't forget.
It keeps reaching for something.

Eight Chinese international students. Eight universities across America — from Atlanta to Boston, Los Angeles to Ithaca. Eight kitchens photographed, eight voices recorded in Mandarin. The question was deceptively simple: what do you eat?

What emerged was far more complicated than appetite. Food is where identity gets negotiated daily — quietly, practically, hungrily. Every grocery run is a small act of self-preservation. Every meal cooked alone is a rehearsal of who you were, and a decision about who you're becoming.

This is not a story about restaurants. It is a story about the space between the stove and the self.

The people interviewed will be known here only by the city where they are and the dish they cannot stop missing.

What the food is really saying
Five Tensions
These are not just eating habits. They are five recurring arguments conducted entirely through what ends up on a plate.
01 ›
Gut Memory
No one misses "Chinese food." They miss a specific fish, a specific vendor, a specific hour of day.
This is not nostalgia. This is the body carrying a spatial and sensory record that no substitute can overwrite. The dish missed is always particular. The grief is always particular. The person in Los Angeles doesn't miss Chinese food — they miss freshwater river fish, the fat of the 鲢鱼头. The person in Ithaca doesn't miss Sichuan food — they miss a specific flavor profile so precisely defined that they can identify a single wrong ingredient in a bowl of 夫妻肺片.
Los Angeles · 5 yearsThe fish here — tilapia, sea bass — it tastes completely different from the river fish back home. 豆腐鱼、鲢鱼头 — I miss them so much. The ingredient itself doesn't exist here.
Ithaca · 6 yearsI ordered 夫妻肺片 and they added 麻酱. That's wrong. It's a small thing. But the kind of wrong that accumulates.
Charlottesville · 5 yearsI've had 凉皮 here. It's good. But it's not the 凉皮 I had on the street in Xi'an. I can't explain why. It just isn't.
02 ›
The Kitchen as the Stable Room
When everything else shifts, the stove follows a logic that is entirely your own.
Six of eight people cook regularly. But the way they describe cooking reveals it is rarely just about efficiency. It is methodical, deliberate, often solitary — and deeply orienting. One person in Atlanta cooks every single meal. Another describes the precise sequence of heating oil, charring peppercorns, removing them before they burn. A third cooks to "recalibrate" after eating 白人饭. The kitchen is the one place in America where the logic is entirely their own.
Atlanta · 2 yearsWhen I've been eating 白人饭 for too long, I come home and cook. It's like recalibrating. Bringing myself back.
Atlanta · 5 yearsHeat the oil. Add the 花椒. Wait until fragrant. Take them out before they turn black. Then the 杏鲍菇. A little soy sauce. That's it.
03 ›
The Alone–Together Paradox
Group delivery chats. Solo desk meals. Community assembles around the logistics of hunger, then disperses to eat alone.
The delivery group chats — 南北通, campus WeChat groups with hundreds of members — create a strange new form of community. The act of ordering is collective; the act of eating is almost always alone. This is not a failure of community. It is a new shape of it — one that only makes sense in diaspora, where belonging is provisional and mealtimes are uncoupled from togetherness.
Charlottesville · 5 yearsIf I order takeout alone, I can only get one dish. But one dish isn't enough variety. And two is too much food. The math of eating alone never works out.
Ithaca · 6 yearsEvery Friday, a few of us would cook together. Each person made one dish. That was the happiest meal of the week — not because the food was great, but because we were all there.
04 ›
Resistance & Resignation
They know the broth isn't the same. They order anyway. That "anyway" carries everything.
Every choice to eat Chinese food in America is simultaneously an act of cultural insistence and an acceptance of imperfect translation. The students know the fish will be different, the spice will be wrong. They order anyway. In that "anyway" lives the refusal to let the category disappear, even when the specific thing within it cannot be fully recovered. The person in Los Angeles who saw bacon cooked in the hot pot for the first time — stunned, then gradually accepting, then eventually joyful — that arc is the entire experience of living between worlds.
Los Angeles · 5 yearsThe first time I saw someone cook bacon in the hot pot, I was stunned. I'm Sichuan — I'd never seen that. But slowly I got used to it. And honestly? Eating together like that was the happiest thing.
Atlanta · 9 yearsI've spent half my life here. But my tastes haven't changed. I don't think they will. That part of me just stayed.
05 ›
Absence as Dislocation
Without familiar food, one person feels like a guest all day. Food is the mechanism for arriving.
One person in Atlanta describes it most precisely: on days when they cannot eat the food they're used to, they feel like a guest — all day, everywhere, even in their own apartment. The word is 做客 — being a guest in someone else's home. The absence of familiar food doesn't just create hunger. It creates a background noise of unbelonging that makes every other task slightly harder. Food is not just fuel. It is the most reliable mechanism for arriving.
Atlanta · 5 yearsIf I haven't eaten something I actually like that day, I feel like a guest the whole day. No moment of coming home and relaxing. That process — eating — is what puts me at ease.
Atlanta · 1 yearEvery time I eat Chinese food, I miss home a little. But I also feel like I've come back to something.

Central Argument
独/共
Alone. Together. Not a contradiction —
an accurate description.

The delivery group chats have hundreds of members. The coordination is collective. The eating is almost always solo. This new form — communal logistics, solitary consumption — is specific to diaspora and to this generation: enabled by WeChat, shaped by the economics of ordering alone.

But the deeper paradox is this: the choice to eat Chinese food in America is simultaneously an act of cultural resistance and an act of resignation. Resistance, because it refuses to passively accept dining hall food as cultural neutrality. Resignation, because the food is always already a translation — the broth approximated, the vegetables substituted, the 麻酱 appearing uninvited. Students know this. Most describe a quiet grief about it — not dramatic homesickness, but a low hum of incompleteness that surfaces most acutely at mealtimes.

What is collective
Group delivery chats. Shared WeChat orders with hundreds of members. Communal cooking on Friday nights. The social architecture of obtaining Chinese food together.
/
What is solitary
Eating at a desk. One portion, alone. The structural awkwardness of ordering for one. The private kitchen of memory, closed to everyone else.

The person in Ithaca's Friday communal cooking stands as the counter-image: what it looks like when logistics and eating collapse into a single moment. The happiest meal of the week. Not because the food was great. Because everyone was there.

A Final Word
家
Eight students. Five years average in the US. Seventy percent of meals still Chinese. Not because they cannot eat otherwise. Because the body, given the choice, reaches for what it knows. That reaching is not weakness. It is the most honest account any of these students can give of who they are — conducted not through language, not through ceremony, but through the daily, private, necessary act of eating.
